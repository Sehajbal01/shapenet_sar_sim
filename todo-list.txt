Reviewer #1 Comments
****************************************
This work describes an open-source, GPU-accelerated SAR simulator in PyTorch that ray-traces arbitrary data.Object meshes with customizable 
radar settings (wavelength, bandwidth, and sampling rate) and simple per-face material features, such as two-bounce interactions. "
Interposummation" is a sinc-based interpolation-and-summation approach that maps discrete energy-range scatterers into a sampled received signal 
commensurate with the system bandwidth/sample rate, followed by spotlight-mode picture generation tests using ShapeNet automobile models.

The work has several strong advantages:
1) High practical value / accessibility: PyTorch + GPU setup is attractive for most research workflows due to already existing infrastructure.
2) Clear end-to-end story: ray tracing → energy–range scatter → interposummation → simulated received signal → SAR image (figures help communicate the flow).
3) Interesting observation about oversampling artifacts: the discussion and diagnostic figure on how higher Fs/Bs can reveal “delta-like” 
discontinuities (and mitigation via ray density or reduced bandwidth) is a useful practical insight for users of such a simulator.

However, I see room for potential improvements:
1) Limited evaluation scope. The experiments are mostly qualitative and focus on a single class of models (cars) and spotlight-mode 
reconstructions. There’s no comparison to an established simulator.
2) Modeling assumptions are simplistic / under-specified. The per-face parameters (reflect/scatter/absorb) are introduced, but the scattering/
backscatter model is quite simplified; it’s unclear how well this matches typical SAR phenomenology (e.g., aspect dependence, specular glints, 
diffuse scattering, etc.)
3) Reproducibility details need tightening. Several parameters in the table appear unitless or unclear (e.g., “spatial bw 90”, “spatial fs 90”, 
wavelength units, geometry scaling). Without consistent units and a clearly stated coordinate system/scale, it’s hard for others to reproduce 
results or port scenes reliably.

Suggested edits:
1) Add coherent validation tests, such as a single point-scatterer or a simple canonical target (flat plate/trihedral) with a known phase 
history and focused response. Demonstrate that complex-valued focussing operates as intended.
2) Compare qualitative structure and/or basic metrics (peak location, resolution, sidelobes) in a single controlled trial that roughly 
corresponds to RaySAR or CVDomes conditions.
3) Open-source preparedness: incorporate (in the paper and repository) the license, minimal sample scripts, and a reproducibility section 
detailing the precise configuration employed to produce significant figures.

Overall, I think the tool is promising and could be very useful to the community, but the paper needs stronger evidence that it produces 
coherently accurate SAR phase histories (not just visually plausible magnitude images), plus clearer specification of units and assumptions.

Reviewer #2 Comments
****************************************
The paper addresses a relevant and timely problem, namely the lack of flexible open-source SAR simulators, and the general idea of combining ray 
tracing with a signal-level model is interesting. However, in its current form, several aspects limit the technical strength and clarity of the contribution.

First, the proposed Interposummation technique is presented as a core novelty, but from a signal processing perspective it appears equivalent to 
a standard convolution with a sinc kernel, or, equivalently, an ideal matched filtering / low-pass filtering operation in range. While applying 
this operation directly to ray-based energy–range scatters is a reasonable implementation choice, the manuscript does not sufficiently clarify 
how this differs conceptually or practically from classical range compression or matched filtering. A clearer positioning with respect to 
established SAR signal models would strengthen the contribution and avoid overstating novelty.

Second, the paper lacks quantitative or qualitative comparisons with existing SAR simulators (e.g., RaySAR, CVDomes, or other physics-based tools
). Although these frameworks are acknowledged as outdated or limited, they are widely known for their physical accuracy. Without side-by-side 
comparisons (either in terms of image quality, signal fidelity, or computational trade-offs) it is difficult to assess the realism and accuracy 
of the proposed simulator beyond visual inspection.

Third, the handling of complex-valued signals raises concerns. The results show that SAR imaging with complex signals performs poorly, while 
magnitude-only imaging yields visually acceptable results. This behaviour is not sufficiently explained and represents a significant limitation, 
given that real SAR imaging fundamentally relies on phase coherence. A deeper analysis is needed to clarify whether this issue arises from the 
signal model, phase handling across rays, numerical implementation, or assumptions in the ray-based scattering model.

Additionally, some analyses and figures would benefit from clearer presentation. For example, Figure 1(c) and Figure 1(d), described as “range 
per ray” and “energy per ray,” visually resemble rendered target images but lack colorbars, scales, or units. As a result, it is difficult to 
interpret these plots quantitatively or to understand how range and energy values are distributed across rays.

Overall, the work addresses an important need and has clear potential, especially as an open-source, GPU-accelerated framework. However, the 
simulator appears to be at an early stage of development. Strengthening the theoretical framing of Interposummation, improving comparisons with 
established simulators, clarifying the limitations related to complex-valued imaging, and refining the presentation would significantly improve 
the paper’s impact and credibility.

Reviewer #4 Comments
****************************************
The manuscript describes an interesting piece of software developed to simulate SAR imaging. Its main claim to novelty is the use of ray tracing 
to consider up to 2 bounces per ray, and the first using PyTorch libraries to work with any 3D .obj model. The manuscript is clearly written, 
and simulations are illustrative. It appears the method requires very detailed 3D models of targets, including full reflection/absorption/
scattering coefficient specified for every triangle of the model. How feasible this is for general purpose SAR imaging is questionable. 
Practical concerns aside, the greatest weakness of the manuscript is the lack of any verification for the correctness of the code, and no 
measurements of performance against benchmarks of any kind. The manuscript would be stronger if some evidence of correction is provided. There 
is undoubtedly some good work, but the paper feels premature for dissemination.
